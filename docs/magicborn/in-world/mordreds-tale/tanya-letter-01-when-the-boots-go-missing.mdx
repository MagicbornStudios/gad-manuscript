# Letter I — When the boots go missing

Mother,

I would not bother you if I had anyone in Tarro who would answer straight without measuring how much truth a wife is allowed to hold.

You remember how Owain talked when we were still on our side of the border — relic hunter, steady work, the Church not as a cage but as a ladder. The **House of Small Mercies** was supposed to be the proof that he could provide: register, descend when the bells allow, come home with clean hands and a purse that does not lie. I crossed for that promise. I crossed because he looked at me the way a man looks at a life he intends to keep.

Lately the life does not feel kept. It feels borrowed.

He leaves in the middle of the night.

Not every night — that would almost be simpler; I could call it a shift, a habit, a rot I could map on a calendar. It comes in clusters. Two nights, then quiet. Then three. I wake to cold sheets and the door’s small sound, wood and latch, the careful pressure of a man who does not want to wake his wife and yet cannot stop himself from going.

When he returns, his boots are sometimes damp from streets I did not hear rain on. Sometimes there is grit on the stair that was not there when I swept at dusk. Once I found a smear on the jamb, dark enough to be blood if I let my mind go there — I scrubbed it until my knuckles hurt and told myself it was mud.

He says *church business*.

He says *relics*.

He says **Jack** — they still call him **the Patron** in the lanes, as if titles were safer than names — needs a steady hand, and who else would Owain be if not steady?

I nod. I make tea he does not drink. I pretend sleep when he slides back in beside me smelling of smoke and something underneath smoke, sweetish, wrong for incense. I lie with my back to him and count his breaths until they slow, and I wonder which part of me is foolish for still loving him and which part is foolish for not screaming.

Something is wrong, Mother. Not the small wrong of a man who drinks too much or flirts at market. I know what that looks like; Hallinghorn taught me the shapes of ordinary shame. This is different. It sits in my teeth like a warning before fever — *danger*, the kind that does not knock and does not explain.

If you have heard anything, even rumor, even gossip you would usually spit on — men who vanish into cellars under patron houses, husbands who come home with keys that were not theirs in the morning — write to me. Write even if you think I will hate the letter. I am already afraid; hatred of truth would be a relief compared to this guessing.

Tell Father I am well enough in body. Do not tell him I am well in soul. He will hear pride in that and I have none left to spare.

Your daughter,  
**Tanya**
