# Letter VI — After

Mother,

The church praises a woman who opens her arms to a child not born of her body. They put that praise in songs. They stitch it into sermons until it sounds like virtue is a single pattern and any woman who cannot match it is damned by default.

I tried.

I tried for longer than I want to admit, because trying felt like the only dignity left — as if I could earn back my own self-respect by being *good* in the way the stories demand.

I am not that woman.

When Enid enters a room, the hair on my arms rises. When Owain speaks gently to her, I hear insult to Geraint — unfair, I know, yet the sound lands in my body before my mind can correct it. When she is careful near my son — guiding his hand to a cup, describing a shape he cannot see — I hear mockery even when her voice is soft, even when she means kindness. Every kindness from her feels like a blade dressed in silk.

I despise her. I am not proud of it. I am not asking you to be proud of me either. I am only honest in ink, where no neighbor can pretend they did not hear what I said if they repeat it wrong.

Part of me remembers she is a child. Part of me remembers I once feared her for gifts she did not choose. Those parts are still in me; they do not win. What wins is the image of Geraint’s eyes — open, useless, searching — and the certainty that sits behind my ribs like a second heartbeat: *her world took something from mine.*

If you write back, do not counsel me to forgive. Not yet. Maybe not ever. Tell me instead if you have ever hated someone and still had to share a table. Tell me how you survived it without becoming a ghost.

**Tanya**
