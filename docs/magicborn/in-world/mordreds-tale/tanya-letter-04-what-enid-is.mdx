# Letter IV — What Enid is

Mother,

You asked once, years ago, what I wanted in a child. I said *healthy* and *loved* and you laughed and said every mother says that until she learns how specific fear can be.

I understand you better now.

The girl is not like other children. I do not mean pretty or clever or quick with a song — Tarro is full of bright little creatures who will break your heart with ordinary gifts. I mean something wrong in the root.

She moves, and the air *listens*. I have watched it in the kitchen when she thinks no one is looking: a draft that follows her hand, a curtain lifting without wind. She hums tuneless scraps and the lamp-flame leans toward her as if the fire prefers her breath to the wick.

When she was smaller — still toddling, still learning the word for spoon — a pot lifted when she reached. Just a finger’s width. Just long enough for the handle to tap the hook. Owain laughed. **Jack**’s people, when they saw it, did not laugh; they *noted*, the way a merchant notes weight.

I have never seen spellcraft in a child’s hands like that. Not in Hallinghorn stories, not in the church lectures Owain drags home about borrowed power and relic law. This is not a bark bracelet dampening a boy’s tongue. This is not discipline and scripture. This is something that should not be *possible* without cost — and yet she walks through the house like cost is a word other people pay.

I am frightened of her. Owain is not. He speaks to her gently, the way you speak to a kitten that has not yet learned claws. The Patron’s circle watches her with a attention I cannot decode — not love, not quite; *interest*, the way men watch a vein of ore open in rock. Sometimes she is fed first. Sometimes she is praised for restraint when my Geraint — my son — is scolded for the same noise. Sometimes a visitor’s eyes rest on her longer than on any other child in the room, as if the rest of us were furniture.

I do not know what they need from her. Nobody tells a wife the whole ledger. I only know what it costs me to see her treated like a small queen while I sweep and carry and bite my tongue until it bleeds.

If you have old tales — anything from the south about children born under wrong stars — send them. I need language for what I am seeing. I need to know if I am wicked for fearing her, or if fear is the only sane thing left.

**Tanya**
