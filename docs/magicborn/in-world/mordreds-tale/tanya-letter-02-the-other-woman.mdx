# Letter II — The other woman

Mother,

Do not read this aloud to the neighbors. Do not fold it into prayer meeting as a lesson about patience. I am not asking for advice; I am asking you to hold what I say the way you hold a hot pan — without dropping it, without pretending it does not burn.

There is a woman people whisper about in Tarro.

Not a wife. Not a servant you can point to in daylight and say, *There, that one, I know her kin.* She lives under language — *detained*, *protected*, *church business* — the way poor people live under a roof that leaks: you learn where not to stand when it rains, but you never forget the sky is open.

They say she is a prisoner under the patron house. They say her mouth does not open, or opens only for the wrong ears. They say her name the way you say a fever’s name, hoping naming will shrink it.

**Morgana.**

I said it once at supper, lightly, as if I were speaking of a saint’s day I did not care for. Owain’s spoon hesitated. Just a breath — not long enough for anyone else at the table to notice — but I am his wife; I have trained myself on his small tells the way a sailor trains on wind.

He said, *Superstition.*

He said, *Gossip.*

He said, *You’ll make enemies repeating tavern stories.*

And then he washed. Not his hands only — his face, his forearms, as if the name had dirtied him and he could not bear to carry it to bed. The water ran long after the soap was gone.

Mother, I am not a fool. I know what men do when power hands them keys. I know what keys can open when a kingdom decides a woman’s body is a ledger entry. I am telling myself I am jealous of nothing real — that Morgana is a story told to frighten wives into silence — and then I watch my husband stand at the basin like he is trying to remove himself from his own skin.

If you write back, do not counsel me to trust. Tell me instead what Hallinghorn says about Tarro’s church when the candles are low. I need one honest sentence from outside this house.

**Tanya**
