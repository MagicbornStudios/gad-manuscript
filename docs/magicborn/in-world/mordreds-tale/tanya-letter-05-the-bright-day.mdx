# Letter V — The bright day

Mother,

Our son is blind.

I will not give you a soldier’s report. I cannot line up the moments clean enough to satisfy a court, and I will not let this letter become evidence for men who want a story that ends in policy instead of blood.

What I can say: there was light where light should not have been — not sun, not lamp — a brightness with an edge, like a shout made visible. Then screaming. Then my hands on Geraint’s face, his small skull shaking under my palms, his eyes open and *seeing nothing*, the pupils trying anyway, the way pupils do, as if hope were a physical reflex.

Afterward the room smelled burned. Not wood smoke. Not dinner. Something thinner, wronger — like lightning had scraped the air and left a wound.

Geraint asks when it will heal.

I tell him the truth because lying would be another kind of theft. I tell him in pieces, the way you feed a hurt animal — small sentences, pauses, water between. He cries until he is empty and then he is quiet in a way that frightens me more than noise.

Mother, he will never see his own face in water. He will never watch a hawk wheel over the market. He will never learn a woman’s expression by watching her eyes soften — that whole grammar of courtship I took for granted because I had it. He will navigate the world by touch and sound and the cruel kindness of people who mean well.

I will never see him become a man the way mothers are promised — the whole arc, the whole ordinary miracle of watching a child turn into someone you can argue with as an equal. Someone took that from him. Someone took that from *me*, and I do not care how the church wraps it in penance and procedure: I know where I lay the blame.

If you pray, pray for my boy’s hands. They will have to learn what his eyes cannot. If you cannot pray, write to me anyway. I need a mother’s voice that is not inside my own head.

**Tanya**
