# Letter III — The child

Mother,

**Enid** came first — Jack’s story, Owain’s open arms, the whole roof trained to make room before I had said *yes* to anything.

Now I am carrying Owain’s son.

I have known for a fortnight — sickness in the mornings, breasts tender, a secret pressed under my ribs that should have been pure joy. I imagined writing you with *Grandmother* ink, names, lying-in, your hands on my belly.

Instead I write you from a kitchen that feels like a courtroom.

The girl is already **here**. Not a cousin. Not a debt explained in full. A child **Jack** calls his — his blood, his mistake, his tidy word — and Owain behaves as if we **needed** her before we needed the child **I** am making.

I asked *why her first*.

I asked *why us*.

I asked why my womb has to compete with a bastard line already eating at my table — *just because*, Mother, as if hunger were a virtue and my belly were not proof enough that this family was still **ours** to build.

Owain looked at me with tired kindness, the kind that hurts worse than shouting, and said we had room. We had room. As if room were only boards and bread and not air, not precedence, not the thin daily respect a wife trades for peace.

I am furious until I shake. By day I smile at the girl because if I do not, people will say I am wicked.

Her name is Enid.

I hate that I know it. I hate that I have to use it. I hate that when she looks at me with a child’s open face, part of me wants to be fair, and fairness feels like betrayal of the son **inside** me who should have been **first** in every eye.

Write to me. Tell me I am not mad. Or tell me I am mad and I will at least know the shape of it.

**Tanya**
