They moved him in a wagon with barred sides "for your safety," the woman said.

Alun stopped believing safety meant what adults said it meant.

When the wheels stopped, he heard chains.

The yard was stone and sky and too many eyes. A second cart waited like an afterthought. His parents stood between soldiers—wrists bound, mouths stiff, clothes that had been fine a week ago now wrinkled and stained.

Alun lunged to the bars.

"Da!" he shouted, shameless with hope. "Mam!"

His mother twisted—awkward, desperate—and looked back at him. Her eyes were wet and fierce, full of something she could not say without making it worse.

His father did not turn.

Alun watched the line of his jaw, the fixed stare at nothing ahead, as if the world had narrowed to a point his son was not allowed to occupy.

"Da!" Alun tried again, smaller.

Nothing.

Kael's gloved hand settled on Alun's shoulder through the slats—not gentle, not cruel. Simply *there*, like a rule made flesh.

"Come," Kael said.

Alun went, because the alternative was to break himself against iron.
