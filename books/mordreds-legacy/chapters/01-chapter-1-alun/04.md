Alun meant to hug him. That was all. Joy still burned in his chest—a clean flame, child-bright.

His father moved like a man catching a falling pot.

The shove was not a hug. The foot came a heartbeat later, quick and trained, catching Alun's ribs and the water together, sending him under.

The river was shallow; it should have been safe.

It still filled his nose and mouth with cold panic. He thrashed, found bottom, coughed, came up streaming and sobbing—not from pain, not entirely, but from the wrongness of it.

When he wiped his eyes, his father was already on the bank.

"Stay," his father said. The word hit like a slap because it was not gentle. It was an order.

Then he walked away—up the path, into the trees, not looking back.

Alun stood in the ford with his hands shaking, the playful bubbles gone, the river ordinary again.

Ordinary was not comforting anymore.
