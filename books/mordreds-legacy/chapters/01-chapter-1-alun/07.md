They did not chain Alun.

They chained his parents.

Alun rode in front of Kael's saddle with a blanket wrapped around him like an accusation he could not understand. The other two Magicborn flanked them, close enough that Alun smelled oiled leather, horse sweat, and something faintly green—like crushed leaves on steel.

The road unspooled, wheel-rutted, indifferent.

"You are not being punished for being a child," Kael said at last, voice even.

Alun said nothing.

"Magicborn law is not village law," Kael continued, as if explaining rain to someone who had only ever lived indoors. "What you did at the river—what answered you—does not belong to games. Not outside sanction. Not without license."

Alun did not know what *sanction* meant. He knew *license* from the mill—permission, paper, stamps.

He knew his father had looked at him like a stranger.

"Where are we going?" Alun whispered.

Kael was quiet long enough that Alun thought he would not answer.

"Where children like you learn what the kingdom needs them to be," Kael said.

That was not an answer.

It was a warning dressed as one.
