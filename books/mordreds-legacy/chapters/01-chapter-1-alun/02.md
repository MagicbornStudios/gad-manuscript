By the spring he turned four, Alun had learned the village the way a puppy learns a yard: where the mud gripped your boots, which dog would wag and which would bite, how the mill bell meant different things at noon than at dusk.

He did not learn why adults lowered their voices when riders passed in House colors.

He learned smaller rules instead: come when you're called, don't stray past the bend, don't repeat strange words you hear in the market.

On the morning his father said they would wash at the river, Alun whooped and hunted for his second-best shirt—the one that could get wet. His mother braided his hair too tight and kissed the top of his head.

"Mind the current," she told his father.

His father smiled the way he always did when he meant *I hear you.* "We'll stay in the shallows."

Alun grabbed his hand and pulled him toward the lane.
