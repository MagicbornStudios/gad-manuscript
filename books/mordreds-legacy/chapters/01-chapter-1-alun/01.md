Before Alun had words, he had warmth.

It arrived in arms that smelled of bread and sweat, in a voice humming off-key, in the slow stroke of a palm along his spine when the world seemed too loud. His mother would lift him to the window so he could watch rain stitch the yard; his father would pretend the lightning was a game, covering his eyes at the bright flash and uncovering them with a grin.

They were not rich. The cottage was one room and a lean-to, smoke in the rafters, a door that stuck in wet weather.

They were happy anyway.

At least—Alun would remember it that way later, when memory became a thing he had to fight for.
