The hammering on the door sounded like the world ending politely.

His mother answered—voice high, controlled. His father said nothing at all.

Alun peered through a knothole and saw three shapes in the yard: tall, armored, the sun catching dark metal. Veins of something like bark threaded the breastplates and the hilts, as if the forest had been forced into obedience and soldered there.

The man in front had a face you would remember if you had to pick it from a line: not cruel, not kind—**finished**.

"Unlicensed display at the ford," he said. "Witnesses."

Alun's mother made a sound like a swallowed scream.

"We saw the boy coming up the lane," the man continued, calm as weather. "Alone. Upset."

Kael—that was the name someone used later, when names mattered—looked past her shoulder into the cottage.

"Both of you will come," he said. "Now."

Iron clicked. His father swore again, softer, like a man realizing the story was over.

Alun pressed his palm to the splintery wood and tried to disappear.
