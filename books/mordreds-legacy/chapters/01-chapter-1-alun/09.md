That night, lying on a pallet between strangers, Alun tried to hold his old life in his hands like a pebble.

His father carrying him when he had fever.

His father teaching him to whistle badly on purpose so Alun could laugh.

His father's hand, huge and warm, ruffling his hair before sleep.

The pebble would not stay one shape.

It kept turning into the ford: the foot, the water in his lungs, the back walking away.

It kept turning into the doorway: his mother's shoulder, the table, the sound.

Alun pressed his face into the wool and wished for an explanation that would make both memories true.

None arrived.

Only the camp noises: coughs, whispers, someone crying quietly with their teeth clenched.

Alun joined them without meaning to.
