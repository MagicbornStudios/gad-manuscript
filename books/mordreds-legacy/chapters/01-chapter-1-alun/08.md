The camp stank of smoke and boiled grain and bodies too busy to wash.

Alun had never seen so many children in metal.

Not play-metal, not pots turned into helmets for games. Real leather, real steel, sizes wrong—too big on the young ones, as if the armor had been inherited before the bones caught up. They moved in lines. They repeated syllables until their throats sounded raw. When one stumbled, an instructor's stick tapped the mud—not always hard, but always *there*.

Alun clutched his blanket.

A girl with a shaved stripe along her scalp glanced at him and looked away, bored, as if new fear were a daily weather.

Kael handed him off to a woman with ink on her fingers and a measuring cord.

"Name?"

"Alun."

She wrote it down.

"Eat," she said, and gave him a bowl.

The porridge tasted like nothing.

That was almost a relief. At least nothing did not lie.
