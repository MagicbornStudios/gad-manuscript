The walk home took forever.

Alun's clothes clung. Mud wicked up his calves. He kept stopping to listen, half-convinced his father would return with an apology and a joke and a dry blanket.

No footsteps followed him except his own.

The cottage door stood ajar. That never happened; his mother hated drafts.

Inside, a sound he had never heard in that house: a wet, sharp cry cut short, then a thud.

Alun froze on the threshold.

Through the gap he saw his mother's shoulder hit the table edge. Saw his father's hand—*the same hand that had steadied him in the river*—closed in her hair.

Alun made a small noise.

His father's head snapped toward the door.

For one terrible second, Alun thought he would come for him next.

Instead his father swore—low, vicious—and shoved his mother aside. She slid to the floor, hand pressed to her mouth, eyes finding Alun through the doorway as if she could shield him with nothing but a look.

Alun ran.

Not away into the fields—there was nowhere. Behind the woodpile. Into shadow. Into breathing as quiet as he could make it.

Not quiet enough.
