The ford was sun-dazzled and cold enough to make Alun yelp, then laugh.

His father rolled his trousers high and waded with him, hands firm under Alun's arms whenever the stones shifted. Minnows stitched silver through Alun's legs. He splashed until his father splashed back, a rare, bright recklessness.

In stories, rivers listened.

Alun had heard those stories whispered—not the loud kind told for crowds, but the kind told like secrets, with glances at doors. He did not understand the politics of listening. He understood play.

He cupped water in his small palms and whispered, earnest as prayer, "Little fish—show me."

For a breath, nothing happened.

Then bubbles rose—not the messy froth of splashing, but a neat spiral, glinting, as if the river had chosen a shape and decided to amuse him. They broke the surface in quiet pops, pearl-like, obedient.

Alun gasped, delighted.

"Da," he said, turning, already running through the water with his arms open. "Da, look, I did it—"

His father's face had gone wrong.

Not anger, not yet. Something worse: a crack, as if the man he loved had been a cup and someone had tapped it once, hard.
