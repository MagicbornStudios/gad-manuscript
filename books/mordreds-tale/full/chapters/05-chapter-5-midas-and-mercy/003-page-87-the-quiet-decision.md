"He's not going to be a child you raise," the clerk said. "He's a debt."

The lay brother didn't argue. He stared at the basket the way men stared at ledgers when the bottom line turned red.

"The patron doesn't want a rival saint in twenty years," he said finally. "He wants a battery that doesn't talk back. If the boy won't bend to that —"

"He's an infant."

"Infants become boys. Boys become problems." The lay brother's voice was flat. "Merris won't do it with her own hands. She's got heaven on her breath. But she won't weep if he stops breathing in his sleep."

Mordred listened without understanding words. He understood tone — the way the air in the room went thin, the way the clerk's foot started tapping.

"We could sell the Midas piece tonight," the clerk hissed. "Say it vanished. Say the child vanished. The river takes everything."

"The river doesn't pay."

"The patron pays for *useful*."

The lay brother looked at Mordred. For a heartbeat, something like pity flickered — useless, inconvenient pity.

"Tomorrow," he said. "If the message still doesn't come, we do it clean. No marks. No story."

Mordred gummed the disk. The metal warmed — not from his mouth alone. Heat leaked from his palm into the stamp of the hand, into the sun, into the old letters as if something inside him had found a door the Fall was supposed to have barred.

The clerk noticed first. His eyes widened.

"That's — it's not supposed to —"

"It's nothing," the lay brother snapped. "Metal holds warmth."

But it wasn't warmth.

It was *permission*.
