That night they moved Mordred again — not to mercy, not to a nursery. To a room closer to the cellar stairs, where the stone sweated and the bark bands were stored in a box like shavings stripped from something that refused to die.

They changed his cord. They left the clay amulet — cheap, harmless, a lie you could touch — and they wrapped his wrist in fresh bark until his voice went small again.

He slept in fits.

When he woke, the heat inside him had learned patience.

Outside, Tarro kept building itself upward, stone on stone, prayer on prayer, lies on lies. Inside the House, men wrote a new line in a ledger: **Midas Touch — active under contact with subject M.**

No one wrote down what *subject M* was.

They didn't have the words yet.

They only had gold where a man used to be — and a sister who knew how to call damnation *profit*.

Mordred's palm opened against the wool.

Nothing turned.

Not yet.

But the relic had spoken once. The well had answered.

And in the years to come, when crowns and choirs argued over what Magicborn meant, they would trace the argument back to a cellar room where two fools thought a god-killer world had made murder easy —

—and found out it had only made power *hungry*.
