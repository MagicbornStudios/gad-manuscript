When it finished, the lay brother still stood — or the thing that had been him stood — a statue with the shape of panic locked inside it. One hand half-lifted, fingers curved as if to snatch back time. Eyes open, pupils gone to molten coin.

The Midas disk clattered to the floor, cord snapped, slick with spit.

Mordred's cry finally tore through — thin, then louder as the bark band popped a stitch and loosened half a finger's width. Sound returned wrong and precious.

The clerk pressed against the door, breathing in gasps.

"We didn't — it wasn't — the relic was *dead* —"

The statue did not answer. It weighed the air differently, like a threat made solid. Gold should have gleamed. This looked *spent*, as if the magic had paid for itself in shame.

Footsteps in the corridor — quick, heavy.

The clerk looked at Mordred, at the open basket, at the disk on the stones.

"If they see —" he whispered. "If they see *him* with —"

He lunged for the child.

Not kindness. Erasure.

Mordred's hand, still clumsy with infancy, struck the clerk's sleeve as the man scooped him up. Cloth — wool — drank the touch for half a heartbeat.

The clerk froze.

Gold spotted the weave like mold.

"No," he whimpered. "No, no, I wasn't — I didn't —"

The spot spread.

He dropped Mordred back into the wool.

The fall knocked breath out of the child — silence, dangerous silence.

The door latch lifted.

Sister Merris stood on the threshold, calm as a blade before it cuts. Her eyes took in the statue, the disk, the clerk on his knees with a sleeve turning precious.

Her mouth tightened.

"Well," she said softly. "Now we know."

She stepped inside and shut the door behind her.
