The lay brother stood. He'd decided — or he'd been decided for — and his body moved before his mouth could make it pretty.

"Give it here," he said. "If it's warming, we lock it back. Last thing we need is a rumor."

He reached into the basket.

Mordred's fingers were tangled in the cord some careful fool had looped through the disk's hole — not the clay amulet at his throat, something uglier, older. The lay brother's hand closed over the relic to pry it free.

Skin touched skin around dead metal.

For a breath, nothing happened.

Then the lay brother's knuckles went dull yellow — not candlelight, not stain. *Metal color*, spreading under living flesh like a tide you couldn't refuse.

He jerked back.

Too late.

The gold ran up his wrist in a sleeve of impossible wealth. It didn't shine like storybook coin. It looked *true* — heavy, final, the way a verdict looks when the seal is pressed.

The clerk screamed.

The scream hit the bark band on Mordred's arm and broke into a thin whistle. He startled, still holding the disk, and the motion dragged the relic across the lay brother's reaching palm.

Gold swallowed the scream next.

It climbed the forearm, the elbow, the shoulder. The lay brother's face contorted — not pain, not yet; *surprise*, the awful surprise of a man realizing his own body had been mistaken for ore.

Mordred began to cry — real sound fighting the bark, ragged and furious.

The clerk backed into the bench and tipped the bottle. Glass shattered. Wine spread like a second kind of red.

"Don't touch him!" the clerk shrieked — to himself, to the room, to no god that would answer.

The lay brother's mouth opened.

Gold filled it.
