The clerk clawed at his sleeve. Fabric tore; gold flaked like scabs and left raw pink flesh beneath — living, shaking, *saved* by cheap wool and terror.

"He — the baby — the relic — Brother Keth —"

"I know who Keth was," Sister Merris said. "I know what he drank. I know what he planned for tomorrow."

She didn't look at the statue again. Some sights trained you not to look.

She picked up the Midas disk with the hem of her apron, as if even cloth might be too much.

"It was inert," she murmured. "Every test. Every bishop's man. Dead as the rest."

Mordred sucked air, hiccupped, found a thread of voice.

Merris's gaze dropped to him.

For a moment, something moved behind her eyes — not mercy, not the performance of mercy. *Calculation* tempered with fear of God.

"The patron," she said, "wanted a well he could cap."

The clerk sobbed. "He'll kill us."

"He'll pay us," Merris corrected. "If we sell the story right. *Inert relic wakes.* *Miracle.* *Danger.*" Her mouth twisted. "And one less drunk fool to split the take."

She nudged the gold statue with her toe. It didn't topple. It was too heavy for decency.

"You'll live," she told the clerk, "if you forget *murder* and remember *witness*. Say Keth touched the relic alone. Say the child was asleep."

"He wasn't —"

"Say it anyway."

She lifted Mordred. His body trembled against her ribs — heat and hunger and whatever he was, humming like a hive.

Through the door, distant, someone laughed in the gate hall — beer smoke, the sound of a kingdom pretending it had rules.

Merris whispered, not to him, not to Heaven — to the thing she could already see coming.

"They'll call you devil," she said.

And this time the word landed where it belonged.
