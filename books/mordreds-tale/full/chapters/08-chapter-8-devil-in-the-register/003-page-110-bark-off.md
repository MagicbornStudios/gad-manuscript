They put him in the cellar lane when the hall was too bright with visitors.

Down there, the air tasted like onions and old water. Down there, nobody pretended the stone was kind.

Mordred pressed his palm to the wall and whispered — not words his nurses used, not prayers he’d been taught — a syllable that wanted to become **heat**.

The **bark** band bit.

The spell died in his mouth like a swallowed ember.

He coughed. His eyes watered. For a heartbeat he was furious in a way infants weren’t supposed to be — a clean, bright fury that felt like *rightness*.

Footsteps.

Merris came with a lamp and a face that could look gentle if you didn’t know her.

"You don’t do that," she said.

Mordred looked at her.

She crouched until they were eye to eye — adult to child, power to power disguised as size.

"We wear the tree," she said. "Not for cruelty. For **safety**. For **souls**. Do you understand?"

He understood she would take supper away if he tried again.

That was its own kind of theology.
