Isolation was bread without butter.

Mordred learned hunger as a lesson: not the sharp empty of true starvation — Merris was careful about that — but the softer cruelty of being **alone** in a room while voices laughed elsewhere, of hearing the House breathe without him.

When the door opened, it was sometimes a sister with porridge and a psalm.

When it was Merris, it was sometimes porridge and a lecture.

When it was Merris on a bad day, it was water and a cloth and a voice that said, *You will thank me later*, as if gratitude could be trained like a dog.

Mordred did not thank her.

He watched her hands.

He watched the way she checked the registry scrap on her belt — not his full line, just a corner, just enough to remind herself what the institution believed he was.

She was not only his enemy.

That was the hard part.

She also stood between him and men who would have sold a **devil** for parts.
