They stood him in a corridor he hadn’t walked before — stone closer, light thinner, air that tasted like old coins.

A **grille** cut the hall in two: iron diamonds, shadow lines, a narrow view into whatever lay beyond.

Mordred saw boots first.

Then a man’s shape — expensive cloth, hands that did not work like sisters’ hands.

Jack.

The name arrived in Mordred’s head without anyone speaking it, the way heat arrives before flame.

Jack stopped on the other side of the iron as if he’d hit a wall he hadn’t expected.

Their eyes met.

Mordred felt something twist in his chest — not recognition, not love — a hook searching for a hole it couldn’t find.

Jack’s mouth opened.

Nothing came out.

On Mordred’s side, Merris’s fingers tightened on his shoulder like a warning and a shield at once.

Jack’s gaze flicked to her — quick, assessing — then back to the boy.

Still silence.

Then Jack turned away, smooth as a knife sliding into sheath, and the corridor emptied like a story with the middle ripped out.

Mordred did not cry.

He did not know why he wanted to.
