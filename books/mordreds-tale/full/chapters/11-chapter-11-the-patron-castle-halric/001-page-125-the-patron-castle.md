**Years after the rope** — Mordred **fifteen**; Enid **fourteen**. Geraint’s world had learned to navigate without a mother’s hand; the castle had learned to speak of **justice** without flinching.

Jack’s seat was not a merchant’s house anymore. It was **stone**, **height**, **bells** — a castle raised by patron coin and church blessing, all of it saying *permanent* in a kingdom that had lived too long on borrowed charge.

Mordred walked its halls with registry ink still staining his cuticles from borrowed names — **ward**, **miracle**, **danger** — and felt the lie itch.

Below the throne wing, old vaults still held a woman who did not speak.

Mordred did not know her name was **Morgana**.

He did not know the man who signed his food was **Jack** in any sense except power.

What he knew was simpler: **arranged** futures arrived like weather.

That morning he had watched **Prince Halric of Hallinghorn** bow in Jack's audience room with more training than thought. Halric was handsome, perfumed, and precise about rank; he let older men place words in his mouth and repeated them like doctrine. He listened upward, not sideways. Women were furniture unless titled, and poor men were weather unless armed.

Jack called him "the south made reasonable." The church steward called him "a bridge." Mordred heard the arithmetic under both names: marry Enid into Hallinghorn, yoke two crowns, and keep the old experiment alive long enough to breed another **well**.

He was being measured for a throne nobody had asked him if he wanted.

The castle made all these measurements feel like kindness.
