The year Tarro learned mercy could be a **sentence** — not in ink first, but in rope.

The gallows stood where market met church steps, a geometry that told you power liked an audience.

**Tanya**’s hands were bound in front of her the way they bound thieves, not wives. She had stopped screaming days ago; what remained was a thin furious pride, the same pride that had made her lift a strap to a child’s eyes.

**Enid** stood in the front rank because the church insisted the **victim** see **justice** finished.

Owain was not allowed to touch his wife goodbye. He stood with soldiers between them and looked like a man who had mistaken heroism for a clean word.

When the drop came, the crowd made a sound — not cheer, not grief — relief that someone else was holding the sin.

Enid did not close her eyes.

She memorized the rope the way other children memorized hymns.
