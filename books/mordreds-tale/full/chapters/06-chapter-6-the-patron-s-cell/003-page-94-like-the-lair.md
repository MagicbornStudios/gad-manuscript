Jack moved the cot himself — a stupid, domestic act, scraping legs on stone — positioning it the way he remembered the chamber’s angle, as if geometry could summon the past.

He laid the bracelet against her palm first.

Nothing.

No faint hum. No greedy drink. No proof.

He tried the ring on her finger — careful, almost reverent, the way he’d once been careful with her skin because skin had been the only honest mechanism.

Cold metal. Cold flesh.

The pendant he pressed to her throat like a physician checking a pulse.

Still nothing.

Morgana breathed. That was the only living thing happening.

Jack leaned close enough to smell soap and fear and the faint salt of milk gone wrong — **gone**, because the child wasn’t in the room, wasn’t in her arms, wasn’t where Jack needed him to be.

"You can hate me," Jack whispered. "You can curse me. But you can **speak**."

Morgana’s lips stayed closed.

Jack’s hand trembled — not from weakness, from the wrong kind of want.

He closed his eyes and tried to **will** the old world back: the dark, the stillness, the sense that he’d discovered a secret the pantheon hadn’t meant for anyone like him.

When he opened them, she was still watching.

"You used to be grateful," he said — and heard how pathetic it sounded, and hated her for making it sound that way.

He tried to force the moment into shape with words, the way men forced metal into dies.

"Say it," he murmured. "Like you mean it."

Morgana didn’t blink.

Jack’s mouth shaped the lines he wanted **from her**, as if hearing them in his own voice could trick fate.

"Thank you for saving me," he whispered. "I love you. We can have everything together."

The cell gave nothing back.

Morgana gave nothing back.

Only her eyes — **there**, **awake**, **refusing**.
