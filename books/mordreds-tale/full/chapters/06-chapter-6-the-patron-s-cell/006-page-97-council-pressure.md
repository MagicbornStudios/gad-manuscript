Later — an hour, maybe two; time moved like tar when you were arranging sin — Derrick arrived without knocking.

He wasn’t rough this time. That was worse.

He stood in Jack’s study with his hands behind his back like a man trying not to touch weapons he already owned.

"The eastern quarter heard a woman scream," Derrick said.

Jack poured wine he didn’t want. "Rumors."

Derrick’s eyes flicked to Jack’s knuckles — scraped, red.

"Rumors keep councils honest," Derrick said. "Or they bury them."

Jack drank. "You want charge."

"I want **proof**," Derrick said. "I want fountains. I want buyers calm. I want Tarro to look like it knows what it’s doing — because right now it looks like a patron who **lost** his trick."

Jack set the cup down.

"The trick," he said carefully, "is being **refined**."

Derrick studied him — not stupid, never stupid.

"You have her," Derrick said. "You don’t have what she **had**."

Jack didn’t answer.

Derrick leaned in. "Fix it, Jack. Or the united councils will fix **you** — and they’ll call it stability."

When Derrick left, the room smelled like wet wool and threat.

Jack stood alone with maps he couldn’t read anymore.

The House had the boy.

The cell had the mother.

And Jack — for the first time since the lair — had **nothing** warm in his hands.

Only plans.
