The cell wasn’t a dungeon in the storybook sense. No rats staged for atmosphere. No iron maiden. Just stone that sweated in summer, a door thick enough to swallow sound, and a slit high enough to remind you sky existed — for other people.

Morgana sat on the cot with her hands folded in her lap like a queen forced to learn patience. Her hair had been washed. Her wrists were unchained because chains were an admission — and Jack still liked to pretend this was civilized.

When he entered, she didn’t stand.

She looked at him.

That was new. In the lair, she had been **still** in a different way — absent, sealed, a miracle you didn’t have to meet eye to eye. Now her eyes were present. Bright with hate, or grief, or both — the kind of brightness that didn’t need magic to cut.

Jack carried a satchel.

Inside: relics he’d moved through Tarro for months — the proof of his name, the reason men called him **patron** instead of parasite. A bracelet. A ring. A pendant that had warmed for buyers and cooled for enemies.

He set them on the small table like offerings.

The metal looked embarrassed in the dim.

"Lie down," he said softly — almost tender, the way a man might speak to a lover if he’d forgotten what tenderness was for.

Morgana didn’t move.

Jack’s smile flickered. "Lie down," he repeated, "like before."

She stayed upright.

Silence filled the room until it pressed on his teeth.

"Please," he added — a joke he didn’t laugh at.

Morgana’s mouth didn’t open.

But her eyes never left his face.
