The letter smelled like incense and dishonesty.

Jack read it twice — once fast, once slow — while Tarro’s morning pressed against his shutters like a debtor at the door. Sister Merris’s hand was neat. Her logic was neater.

*The infant remains under choir seal. The relic event confirms volatility. Moving the child now risks crown scandal and patron liability. Await the bishop’s word.*

There was no bishop’s word. There was only **wait**.

Jack crumpled the edge of the parchment without meaning to. He smoothed it again. A man who couldn’t control paper couldn’t control a kingdom — and he knew which one he was pretending to be.

Owain stood by the door, helm under his arm, face trained to nothing.

"The boy?" Owain asked.

"Miraculous," Jack said. "Dangerous. **Useful.**" He almost laughed. The laugh would have been worse than rage. "They’re hiding him behind God so they can price him later."

Owain’s throat moved. "We could take him."

"We could start a war with the church before I’ve finished buying the council." Jack set the letter down. "Send another purse. Send pressure. Not steel. Not yet."

Owain nodded — relief, maybe. Maybe cowardice wearing a uniform.

Before either could speak again, a second knock came: council courier, mud-splashed, mouth tight.

"Derrick wants his fountain running by sundown," the courier said. "His words, not mine."

Jack took that message the way he took everything now — as weight.

"Tell Derrick the patron needs **quiet** to work," Jack said. "Tell him **charge** is coming."

It was a lie until it wasn’t.

He went downstairs anyway.
