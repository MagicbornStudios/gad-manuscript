Owain checked the ring twice because his hands were not steady enough to trust once.

The keys hung from a chain at his belt like a joke about mercy: small teeth for small doors, a larger one for the stair that swallowed sound, and the one that made his palm sweat — the one the Patron had pressed into his hand without looking at him, as if love could be transferred by metal.

They did not belong on any form Tanya could read. They were not the kind of keys a **relic hunter** showed at the **House of Small Mercies** gate when he checked in for a licensed descent — the life Owain had promised her when she left Hallinghorn: registry stamps, convoy schedules, **delver** rosters, the clean theater of a man who earned his house by **escorting charge-sensitive cargo** and co-sealing chapel handoffs. That story was still true enough to repeat. It was still the skin he wore in daylight.

The skin had a second throat underneath.

Jack’s order still sat folded in his glove — paper edges biting his palm — the same hand that, in the official tale, only ever held wax seals and tally sticks.

Tarro’s afternoon leaned on the alley wall. Beer and smoke, same as every day the church pretended it didn’t run a throat into the earth. Upstairs, men laughed about **relics** the way men laugh about money: greedy, careful, proud.

"You’ll do it clean," Jack had said. Not a question. A schedule.

Owain had said yes because that was what you said when you wanted to be a hero and knew you weren’t.

He went down.
