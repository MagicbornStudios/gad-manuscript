The room had no windows. That was the first kindness it offered — no sky to remind him of arms that were gone.

Mordred lay on folded wool that scratched. The band at his wrist still itched; when he tried to wail, the sound came out wrong, throttled, as if the bark had teeth.

Two men shared a bench and a bottle.

The younger one had ink on his fingers — registry clerk off-shift, sleeves rolled, eyes too bright. The older one — Keth, keys jingling when he shifted — wore a lay brother's cord but drank like a soldier.

"The stipend for keeping him," the clerk said, "doesn't cover what the cellar eats."

"The cellar eats what I say it eats." The lay brother took a pull. "And Sister Merris says we keep him alive until the patron's man comes back with word."

"Word was supposed to be last week."

"Word is late. The child isn't."

Mordred's fist opened, closed. Hunger gnawed — not sharp enough to kill the heat behind his sternum. That heat had no name. It only waited.

The lay brother set the bottle down and crossed to a chest chained in the corner. Metal whined. He lifted out a cloth bundle like something stolen from a funeral, unwrapped it, and laid the thing inside on the table.

It was a disk of tarnished metal — palm-sized, stamped with a hand and a sun too stylized to be friendly. Old script crawled the rim. Even Mordred's infant sight caught the way the clerk leaned away.

"The Midas Touch," the clerk whispered, as if the walls might sell the name. "Every fence in Tarro would cut a thumb for it."

"Every fence can cut their own thumbs." The lay brother tapped the disk with a knuckle. It rang dull — dead, ordinary. "Fall killed it. It's a story on a plate. But the story still opens purses if you show it right."

He slid it closer to the basket.

"Let him play," he said. "If he shuts up, we can think."

Mordred's fingers found cool metal. The disk was heavier than the clay on his throat. He pulled it to his mouth. The taste was wrong — not food, not milk — something that hummed against his gums like a secret trying to become a tooth.

The clerk watched, nervous and greedy.

"See?" the lay brother said. "Harmless."

Outside the door, somewhere in the House, a bell tolled once — not prayer. Inventory.
