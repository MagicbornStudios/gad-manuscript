The world was warmth and motion, then jolt, then cold air that tasted wrong.

Mordred did not know his name yet. He knew the drum behind his ears — *thump-thump* — and the voice that had lived against his cheek until it tore away in a scream he could still feel in his bones. He knew hunger as a hole, not a word.

The wagon rolled. Wood groaned. Somewhere above, men spoke in low chunks of sound that meant nothing and everything.

When they lifted him, rough cord brushed his throat. A weight hung there: cheap clay, knotted tight, scratching whenever he twisted. It smelled like river mud and someone else’s fear.

The gate smelled different.

Smoke first — not cook-fire smoke, but the thin kind that clung to wool and hair after too many pipes in too small a hall. Under it, yeast and spilled beer, sharp enough to cut the sweet rot of the street. A building swallowed them, and the light went yellow and close.

“Name of the child,” someone said.

A pause.

“Mordred,” another voice answered, as if naming were a kind of claim.

Feet shuffled on stone. Quills scratched. Mordred’s fist found the cord again and pulled; the knot only bit.

A woman leaned into his view — not the nurse from the courtyard, not the guard. Older lines, calm mouth, eyes that weighed him the way a butcher weighed a lamb.

“I am Sister Merris,” she said softly. “You’re hungry. That’s honest. Hunger is the first prayer the body learns.”

She did not smile when she said it.

They carried him deeper. The air cooled. Somewhere a door shut, and the world’s noise dropped away as if the House had eaten it.

Rough fingers worked at his wrist. Something dry and flat circled his skin — not metal, not cloth. It itched like bark stripped from a living thing. When he drew breath to cry, the sound came out thin, wrong, as if the band had stolen part of his voice before he knew he had one.

Sister Merris murmured something that might have been blessing or inventory.

“Welcome,” she said, “to the House of Small Mercies.”

She handed him off down a corridor that smelled of onions and lye. A door shut. The footsteps that had carried him walked away — not all of them, not far.

The dark behind his eyelids began to fill with a heat that did not belong to milk, or arms, or sleep.

Something in him *twitched* toward wakefulness.

Not yet. Not fully.

But the House had already begun to measure what he cost — and what he might be sold for, if he lived long enough.
