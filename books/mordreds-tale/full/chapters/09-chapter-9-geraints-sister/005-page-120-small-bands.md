The bark band for an infant had to be cut small, softened at the edges, checked daily for rash.

Owain fixed Enid’s himself while Tanya looked away.

"This is for **safety**," he said — the church phrase, the House phrase, the lie that made control sound like care.

The wood smelled like the Tree of Tarro: bitter, alive, unwilling.

Enid fussed once, then stilled as if her body recognized restraint.

Jack had sent a sister to instruct them — brief visit, brisk hands, a prayer muttered like a receipt.

When she left, Tanya said, "They treat her like a relic."

Owain didn’t answer.

He couldn’t.

Because the answer was **yes**.

Not the kind delvers bought and sold — something worse: a living object the kingdom could **charge** through, a warm palm pressed to cold metal while adults pretended it was nurture.

Owain told himself he was protecting his household.

He told himself heroism was still available if he squinted.

Geraint watched from the doorway, thumb in mouth, eyes sharp.

Owain wondered what the boy was storing away for later.
