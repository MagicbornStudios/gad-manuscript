That night Tanya fed Enid because the neighbors would notice if she didn’t.

Owain watched her do it with the efficiency of a woman performing mercy she did not feel.

When Jack was gone and the children slept, she set the cup down hard.

"Say it again," she demanded.

Owain knew what she meant.

"Jack’s blood," he said — the cover story, the public skin. "He won’t raise a bastard in his own house. We take her, we keep his name clean, we keep **your** table full."

Tanya laughed once, joyless.

"My table."

"Tanya—"

"Don’t." She wiped her mouth as if the word had dirtied her. "Don’t speak to me like I’m a fool. I know what men are. I know what patrons are. I just didn’t think you’d bring it **home** and ask me to **nurse** it."

Owain had no honest sentence that would not cut her worse.

He reached for her hand.

She pulled away.

In the cradle, Enid breathed — innocent in the way only the used can be innocent: unaware of the price attached to her skin.

Owain thought of Morgana’s silence.

He thought he deserved every inch of his wife’s hatred.

He went on loving the infant anyway, because love was also a habit Jack had trained into him.
