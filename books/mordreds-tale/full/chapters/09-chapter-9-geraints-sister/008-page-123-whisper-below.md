Market gossip reached the house the way smoke reaches lungs: inevitable.

A woman in the lane said the patron still kept a witch under his stones — not dead, not free, a rumor with teeth.

Owain’s hands went cold on the harness.

Tanya watched him from the window.

He did not know how much she believed.

He knew she was listening for the shape of his flinch.

That evening, when the children were down and the lamp low, she said quietly, "Is she real?"

Owain could have lied cleanly.

He chose a smaller lie — the kind that preserves a marriage by bleeding it slowly.

"There are always prisoners," he said. "Tarro runs on them."

Tanya’s eyes glittered.

"That’s not an answer."

"It’s the truest one I have."

She turned away.

Outside, bells marked another Tarro night — delvers, clerks, patrons, the bright machinery of a kingdom that ate silence and called it order.

Owain thought of a grille he had never seen and a boy he could not hold.

He thought of Enid’s palm on metal.

He thought of Morgana listening for footsteps.

And he understood, with a clarity that felt like dying, that the story was not going to save any of them — only delay which wound opened first.
