Jack didn’t do it in the main room.

He waited until Owain was alone with the infant — a calculated intimacy, the patron’s habit of arriving when witnesses were few.

The relic was small: a disk, dull until it wasn’t.

Jack placed Enid’s palm to it with the gentleness of a priest and the focus of a merchant testing weight.

Owain’s skin crawled.

"Feel that?" Jack murmured — not to Owain, to the baby.

Heat rose, faint, wrong in a room with no fire.

Jack’s mouth curved. "There she is."

Owain wanted to pull the child away.

He did not.

He told himself it was the same as the bark — protection, church order, the machinery that fed Tarro.

He told himself he was still a **relic hunter** in a kingdom of relics, only the relics had started to look like daughters.

Enid blinked, solemn.

The metal cooled again, satisfied in a way metal should never be.

Jack tucked the disk away. "Again next week," he said lightly, as if he’d scheduled a lesson in embroidery.

Owain nodded.

His nod was the same nod he’d given in the cell.

That similarity was not lost on him.
