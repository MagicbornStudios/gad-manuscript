Morning came with drums—soft at first, then enough to pull every bunk upright without an order.

Kael dressed by habit: Host tunic, belt, the little disk warm against his sternum from sleep. The oak at his back still felt like ordinary wood when he brushed it with his fingertips. *Not yet,* he thought, and hated that the phrase sounded like promise.

The parade ground had been dressed overnight. Stakes. Ropes. A platform low enough that the front ranks would see every twitch.

Captain Arren stood with the statute unrolled—real vellum, bark-inked, the kind of document that made a crowd quiet because paper, here, was another kind of blade. "Magicborn Host," he said, voice flat with practice. "Item seven. Failure to reconcile armory issue within the window of return."

The man on the platform was older than Kael, cheeks hollow, eyes already elsewhere. Kael did not know him. That should have helped.

It did not.

They read the ingot's tally as if the metal had committed the crime. They read the missing hours. They read the name—**Vorn**—and Vorn's mouth shaped something that might have been excuse or prayer; Kael could not hear it over the wind and the blood in his own ears.

When the line tightened, Vorn did not struggle. Maybe he had spent the night practicing dignity. Maybe he was already dead and the body had not noticed.

Kael watched because everyone watched. That was the design: your eyes were part of the inventory too.

After, when the drums stopped and the ranks broke, Kael walked back to the barracks in a line of boys who smelled like vomit and soap. Someone laughed too loud and was silenced by a look.

In the narrow space beside his bunk, Kael pressed his palm to the oak again.

This time he did not wait for an answer.

He packed what would not rattle: flatbread, the small coin he was not supposed to have, the disk he was never supposed to lose. He left the Host jacket folded like a confession on the pallet.

At the ward-line he did not speak. He let the guard's boredom do the work—two Host boys leaving on a firewood detail, papers smudged just enough to look real if nobody cared.

By midday he was on the trade road with ash still stuck under his nails, and Tarro's walls at his back like a sentence he had not finished reading.

He did not carve anything.

Not yet.

But he knew the shape of the word he would need when the wood finally listened.
