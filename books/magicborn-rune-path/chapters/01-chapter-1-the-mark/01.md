At seventeen, Kael had learned to love the mess hall in the way soldiers learn to love anything that stays warm: not because it was kind, but because it was **predictable**.

Steam lifted from the porridge trough. Someone had traded favors for pepper; the joke went that pepper was the Host's true religion. Kael sat with his squad on the long bench, shoulder to shoulder, the way the roster liked you to sit—close enough to count, never quite close enough to be family.

"Eat," said Jory, older by two wars, if wars could be counted like birthdays. "You'll want a full stomach before the hole."

The **hole** was what they called the Tarro dungeon when they were trying not to make it sound holy.

Kael dipped bread, chewed, swallowed. The duty bell had not rung yet. That was its own small mercy: a few minutes where the day was still only food and noise and the ordinary ache of being young in armor.

On the wall behind the quartermaster's window, a fresh broadsheet hung—ink still sharp, bark-seal stamped in the margin. Kael did not need to read every line. He had memorized the rhythm of those statutes the way other boys memorized songs.

**Issue. Carry. Return. Reconcile.**

The Host did not lose relics.

The Host did not **pretend** it lost relics.

If you tried, the law did not bother with kinship courts.

"You listening?" Jory asked.

Kael nodded. "I'm listening."

Outside, the stump's shadow cut the yard at a wrong angle—too wide, too patient—like something that had been punished and refused to learn the lesson.
