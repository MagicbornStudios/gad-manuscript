The armory wicket smelled of oil, bark-ink, and fear kept tidy.

Kael stepped up when his name was called. The quartermaster did not look at his face; the quartermaster looked at the roster slip as if faces were hobbies and paper was truth.

"Issue for Sortie Seven," the man said. "Dungeon corridor **Garland-4** through **Garland-6**, reclamation priority, hostile contact **likely**."

He laid objects on the counter one by one, each arrival clicking like a small verdict.

A bronze disk—dull until you fed it breath, then briefly honest. A coil of wire meant to **sniff** resonance in stone. A strip of bark-bound leather to strap the disk against the ribs, where the heart's rhythm could steady the charge without letting it run wild.

Kael knew the theory. He had passed the drills. Knowing did not stop his mouth from going dry.

"You sign," the quartermaster said.

Kael signed. The ink bit into the page with a faint greenish flare—**bark-ink** taking a bite of promise.

"If it is not returned," the quartermaster recited, bored as a litany, "you do not argue. You do not bargain. You declare fault before reconciliation closes. Do you understand?"

"Yes."

"If you return late—"

"Death," Kael said, because the word was less heavy when you said it yourself.

The quartermaster's eyes flicked up once, sharp with assessment. Then he nodded, satisfied in the cruel way instructors were satisfied when a boy stopped flinching at the lesson.

"Move."
