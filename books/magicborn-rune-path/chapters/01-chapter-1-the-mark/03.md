The descent always began as architecture and ended as **throat**.

First came the dressed stone of the Host's own making—clean angles, mageglass lamps in iron cages, the confidence of a kingdom that believed it could civilize a wound. Then came older work: seams where older hands had stacked blocks without caring about matching color, as if the mountain had been taught manners in layers.

Then came the **lair**—not all at once, but like a mouth opening by inches.

Kael smelled it before he saw it: sap and cold earth and something sweet-rotten that the scribes called **spoil** and the veterans called **dinner**.

Garland-4 had been cleared six weeks ago, according to the map. "Cleared" did not mean safe. It meant the last patrol had burned out a nest and shored a ceiling and left markers for the next shift to finish being afraid.

Scaffolds rose through a hollowed gallery where roots the width of wagons had been **cut** and **capped** and **wired** with copper like a patient in surgery. Daylight did not belong here; what light they had was deliberate—lanterns, crystals, the faint green gleam of monitoring runes brushed onto support beams.

A team of miners and masons worked one level down, chiseling toward a relic pocket flagged by sniffers. Their curses echoed, human and small.

Kael's squad moved in formation because formation was how you stayed sane.

Jory at front. Kael second. The twins on the flank. A bark-auditor behind them like a shadow with a clipboard.

Somewhere deeper, something scraped stone.

Everyone pretended not to hear it.
