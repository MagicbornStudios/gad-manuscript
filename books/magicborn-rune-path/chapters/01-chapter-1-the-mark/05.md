They found the relic pocket an hour later: a cavity behind a false wall of root-matter, packed with soil and broken offerings.

What came out was smaller than Kael expected—an ingot black as a closed eye, wrapped in chain that had fused with bark-fiber over centuries. It pulsed once when the bark-auditor touched it with tongs, then went still again, as if it had decided to behave for witnesses.

"Bag it," the auditor said.

Kael held the bag open. The weight settled like a child.

On the walk back, he could feel the issued disk against his ribs and the ingot's muffled presence in the pack and the eyes of his squad on him—**not** because they distrusted him, but because everyone distrusted **luck**.

At the ward-line, the surface air hit his face like a slap of ordinary life.

The armory wicket waited.

Kael placed the disk on the counter. The quartermaster struck the line, fed the bark-ink mark until it **closed**, and nodded once.

Alive, Kael thought—stupid with relief—in a kingdom that counted objects more carefully than boys.

That night, when the barracks slept, he pressed his palm to the oak behind his bunk and felt nothing.

Not yet.

But he remembered how marks were made.

And he wondered, for the first time, what it would cost to leave one that belonged to **him**.
