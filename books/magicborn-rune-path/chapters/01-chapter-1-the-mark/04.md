Hostiles came as a rush—not many, but fast, the way rats boil out of a wall when the plaster finally gives.

Kael's training took his body before his fear could. He set his feet, spoke the syllable the disk wanted, felt the bronze warm against his sternum like a second pulse. The wire in his gloved hand lifted, trembling, pointing toward motion in the dark beyond a half-collapsed arch.

**There.**

Not a man. Not quite an animal. A shape with too many joints and a sound like wet leather tearing. It lunged.

Jory's blade caught it high. The thing shrieked—almost speech, almost mockery—and twisted aside.

Kael fed the disk another breath and **pushed**.

The push was not glory. It was ugly work: pressure against a body that should not exist, forcing it to hesitate long enough for steel to finish the argument.

When it fell, the gallery went quiet except for breathing and the drip of something Kael did not examine.

"Mark it," Jory said.

Kael marked it—chalk on stone, Host code, the kind of notation that told the next shift where death had happened and what kind.

His hands shook afterward.

He told himself it was only cold.
